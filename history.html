HISTORY

Evolution of Automotive Excellence

Timeline of Legends

Journey through decades of automotive innovation and racing glory

1965

Shelby Cobra 427

Carroll Shelby unleashes the 427 Cobra, combining American V8 power with British chassis design. The lightweight roadster becomes an instant icon of raw performance.

1966

Ford GT40 Le Mans Victory

Ford achieves its goal of defeating Ferrari at Le Mans with a historic 1-2-3 finish. The GT40 begins its four-year reign of dominance in endurance racing.

1973

Porsche 911 Carrera RS 2.7

Porsche introduces the Carrera RS 2.7 with its iconic ducktail spoiler. The lightweight homologation special sets new standards for sports car excellence.

1987

Ferrari F40 Unveiled

Enzo Ferrari's final creation debuts as a 40th-anniversary celebration. The twin-turbo supercar becomes the first production car to exceed 200 mph.

1989

Nissan Skyline GT-R R32

The GT-R nameplate returns with revolutionary AWD technology. "Godzilla" begins its dominance of Japanese and Australian touring car racing.

1992

McLaren F1 Launch

Gordon Murray's vision of the ultimate road car becomes reality. The F1's central driving position and naturally aspirated V12 redefine automotive perfection.

1995

McLaren F1 GTR Le Mans Win

A modified F1 GTR wins Le Mans on its first attempt, marking the last victory by a car derived from a road-going design.

1999

Mitsubishi Lancer Evolution VI TME

The Tommi Mäkinen Edition celebrates four consecutive WRC titles. Advanced AWD and active yaw control bring rally technology to the street.

Racing Heritage

Every car in our collection shares a common thread: competition. Whether dominating Le Mans, conquering rally stages, or setting production car speed records, these machines were born from the crucible of motorsport.

The technologies and innovations developed through racing competition found their way into these production cars, creating a direct lineage between race track and public road. This heritage of competition is what makes these cars special – they weren't designed to be comfortable or practical, they were designed to win.

Today, these legends represent a golden age of automotive design when engineering prowess, driver skill, and raw mechanical excellence mattered more than electronic aids and computer control. They remain the benchmark by which all performance cars are measured.
